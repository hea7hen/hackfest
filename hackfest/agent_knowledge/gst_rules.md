## SECTION 1: GST 2.0 SLAB STRUCTURE (Effective September 22, 2025)

### GST Slab: 0% — Nil Rated (Essential Life Support)
- Fresh produce (unbranded), milk, bread
- Health insurance (individual policies)
- Life insurance (individual policies)
- Education services
- **Rule:** No GST collected, no ITC available on inputs

### GST Slab: 5% — Merit Rate (Common Use Goods)
- Packaged food and branded staples
- Medicines and pharmaceutical products
- Apparel and footwear priced up to ₹2,500
- Agricultural machinery
- Hotel tariffs under ₹7,500 per night
- Ride-hailing (Uber, Ola) for business transport
- Food delivery (Zomato, Swiggy) — SAC 9963

### GST Slab: 18% — Standard Rate (Mainstream Commercial)
- **Professional services** (most critical for freelancers)
- IT development and software services
- Electronics: laptops, TVs, refrigerators, air conditioners
- Telecom and internet services
- Small cars and motorcycles under 350cc
- Cloud hosting, SaaS platforms
- Co-working / shared workspace rental
- Digital marketing and advertising

### GST Slab: 40% — De-Merit Rate (Luxury and Sin Goods)
- Premium vehicles (above threshold)
- Tobacco products
- Aerated sugary beverages
- Online gaming winnings
- Casino admissions

## SECTION 4: OIDAR — FOREIGN SAAS AND REVERSE CHARGE MECHANISM (RCM)

### What is OIDAR?
Online Information and Database Access or Retrieval services. Post Finance Act 2023, virtually all automated digital services from foreign providers fall under OIDAR. The "minimal human intervention" clause was removed — so AWS, Zoom, Adobe, Canva, Google Workspace all qualify.

### Place of Supply Rule for OIDAR
If the consumer (freelancer) is in India → Place of Supply = India → Indian GST applies.

### GST Liability by Transaction Type

| Scenario | GST Mechanism | Who Pays |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Foreign SaaS → Unregistered Indian individual (B2C) | Direct Collection | Foreign supplier collects 18% IGST and remits to India |
| Foreign SaaS → GST-Registered Indian business (B2B) | Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) | Indian freelancer/business pays 18% IGST directly to government |
| Indian SaaS → Foreign client (Export) | Zero-Rated Supply | 0% GST, subject to LUT filing and forex receipt |

### RCM Obligations for Registered Freelancers
- **Mandatory:** If you are GST-registered and use AWS, Zoom, Adobe, Canva, Google Workspace, etc. — you MUST pay 18% GST under RCM.
- You can then claim this as Input Tax Credit (ITC) to offset your outward GST liability.
- Ignoring RCM on foreign subscriptions is a common audit failure and attracts penalties.
- File RCM liability in GSTR-3B under "Tax on reverse charge."

## SECTION 5: INPUT TAX CREDIT (ITC) FOR FREELANCERS

### What Qualifies for ITC
- Cloud services (AWS, GCP, Azure) — SAC 998315 — 18% recoverable
- Software subscriptions used for business (Adobe, Canva, Figma) — 18% recoverable
- Co-working space rent — 18% recoverable
- Internet / telecom (Airtel, Jio business plans) — 18% recoverable
- Electronics (laptops, monitors) purchased for business — 18% recoverable (post GST 2.0, moved from 28%)

### What Does NOT Qualify for ITC
- Food and beverages (Zomato, Swiggy) — blocked under Section 17(5)
- Personal insurance (not business-related)
- Vehicles for personal use

### ITC on RCM Payments
GST paid under RCM on foreign SaaS qualifies as ITC and can be offset in the same month's GSTR-3B. Net cash outflow = zero if outward liability exists.

## SECTION 6: INCOME TAX ACT 2025 — KEY CHANGES

### Terminology Change: Assessment Year → Tax Year
- Old system: Income earned in "Previous Year" (PY), assessed in "Assessment Year" (AY)
- New system: Single "Tax Year" (TY) — the period in which income is earned
- TY 2026-27 = April 1, 2026 to March 31, 2027
- PAN and TAN remain valid and unchanged

### Act Structure
- Old Act: 800+ sections
- New Act: 536 sections
- Effective Date: April 1, 2026
- Explanations and provisos integrated into main text for clarity

### Critical Update: Manpower Services Under Section 194C
- "Supply of manpower services" is now explicitly defined as "work" under 194C
- Rate: 1% (Individual/HUF) or 2% (Others) — NOT the 10% professional rate
- Resolves long-standing litigation about whether manpower = professional services

### For Freelancers Receiving Payments
- If a client pays you for professional/technical services above ₹30,000 → they deduct 10% TDS under Section 393 (formerly 194J)
- Update your invoicing software to reference Section 393 (not 194J) from April 1, 2026

## SECTION 8: ADVANCE TAX — TAX YEAR 2026-27

### Who Must Pay Advance Tax
- Any taxpayer with estimated net tax liability (after TDS/TCS credits) exceeding ₹10,000
- Governed by Sections 403–410 of Income Tax Act 2025 (formerly Sections 207–219)

### Quarterly Installment Schedule

| Due Date | Cumulative Payment Required |
|----------|----------------------------|
| June 15, 2026 | 15% of total tax liability |
| September 15, 2026 | 45% of total tax liability |
| December 15, 2026 | 75% of total tax liability |
| March 15, 2027 | 100% of total tax liability |

### Grace Period
Payments made until March 31, 2027 are treated as advance tax, but interest may apply if 100% was not paid by March 15.

### Special Rule for Presumptive Taxation (Section 58 / formerly 44ADA)
- Freelancers under Section 58 (44ADA) can pay 100% of tax liability in a single installment by March 15.
- No quarterly breakdown required.
- Eligible if gross professional receipts ≤ ₹75 lakh and at least 50% declared as income.

## SECTION 9: PENALTIES FOR NON-PAYMENT OF ADVANCE TAX

### Section 424 (formerly 234B) — Shortfall in Total Advance Tax
- Triggered if less than 90% of assessed tax is paid via advance tax + TDS by March 31
- Penalty: 1% per month on the shortfall amount

### Section 425 (formerly 234C) — Installment-Specific Delays
- Penalty: 1% per month for a 3-month block for first three installments
- Penalty: 1% per month for 1-month block for the final (March) installment

### Relief Mechanism (Early Estimation Errors)
No interest under Section 425 if:
- At least 12% paid by June 15 deadline (instead of required 15%)
- At least 36% paid by September 15 deadline (instead of required 45%)
This protects freelancers whose income is heavily back-loaded in the financial year.

## SECTION 10: DEDUCTIONS UNDER INCOME TAX ACT 2025

### Section 123 — Investment Deductions (formerly Section 80C)
- **Aggregate limit: ₹1.5 lakh per year**
- Eligible instruments:
  - Public Provident Fund (PPF) — tax-free interest, 15-year maturity
  - Equity Linked Savings Scheme (ELSS) — 3-year lock-in, market-linked
  - Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana (SSY) — for girl child, tax-exempt returns
  - Five-Year Tax-Saving Fixed Deposits (scheduled commercial banks)
  - Home Loan Principal Repayment (only after construction/purchase completion)
- **Additional ₹50,000 for NPS contributions** → Effective ceiling = ₹2,00,000

### Section 126 — Healthcare and Insurance (formerly Section 80D)
- **Self, Spouse, Children:** Up to ₹25,000 for insurance premium + ₹5,000 sub-limit for preventive health check-up
- **Parents (below 60):** Additional ₹25,000
- **Parents (above 60 / Senior Citizens):** Additional ₹50,000
- **Maximum if both taxpayer and parents are senior citizens:** ₹1,00,000

| Beneficiary | Insurance Premium | Preventive Check-up | Max Deduction |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Self, Spouse, Children | ₹25,000 | ₹5,000 (sub-limit) | ₹25,000 |
| Parents (below 60) | ₹25,000 | Included | ₹25,000 |
| Parents (above 60) | ₹50,000 | Included | ₹50,000 |

### Section 133 — Charitable Donations (formerly Section 80G)
- Deductions available at 100% or 50% relief, subject to qualifying limit of 10% of Adjusted Gross Total Income (AGTI)
- **Mandatory digital process:**
  1. Recipient NGO/trust must file Form 10BD
  2. Donor receives Form 10BE (system-generated certificate)
  3. Without Form 10BE, claim will be auto-disallowed by tax department
- **Cash limit:** Donations above ₹2,000 must be via UPI, Net Banking, or Cheque to qualify

## SECTION 11: TAX REGIMES (NEW vs OLD)

### New Tax Regime (Default from TY 2026-27)
- Basic exemption limit: ₹4 lakh
- Lower tax rates across slabs
- Most deductions (Section 123, 126, 133) NOT available
- Simpler compliance

### Old Tax Regime (Opt-in)
- Basic exemption limit: ₹2.5 lakh (general), ₹3 lakh (senior citizens)
- All deductions available (Section 123, 126, 133, HRA, LTA, etc.)
- Better for freelancers with high deductible investments and insurance

### Presumptive Taxation — Section 58 (formerly 44ADA)
- Eligible: Professionals (doctors, lawyers, engineers, architects, accountants, consultants, IT professionals)
- Condition: Gross receipts ≤ ₹75 lakh (or ₹37.5 lakh if cash receipts >5%)
- Deemed income: 50% of gross receipts (no need to maintain detailed books)
- Benefit: Single advance tax installment by March 15

## SECTION 12: GST REGISTRATION AND COMPLIANCE FOR FREELANCERS

### Registration Threshold
- Mandatory GST registration if annual turnover exceeds ₹20 lakh (services)
- Voluntary registration possible below threshold (beneficial for ITC claims on B2B exports)

### Export of Services (Foreign Clients)
- Classified as "Zero-Rated Supply"
- Two options:
  1. File LUT (Letter of Undertaking) → export without paying GST, claim ITC refund
  2. Pay IGST on export → claim refund later
- **Mandatory:** Receive payment in foreign exchange within 9 months
- Payment must be in convertible foreign currency via banking channels

### Filing Obligations
- **GSTR-1:** Monthly/quarterly — outward supplies (invoices raised)
- **GSTR-3B:** Monthly — summary return + tax payment (including RCM)
- **GSTR-9:** Annual return
- **HSN/SAC mandatory** on invoices for turnovers above ₹5 crore (4-digit) and above ₹1.5 crore (mandatory SAC for services)

## QUICK REFERENCE: MOST COMMON FREELANCER TAX SCENARIOS

### Scenario A: Indian client pays ₹50,000 for software development
- GST applicable: 18% (SAC 998314) = ₹9,000 GST on invoice
- TDS deducted by client: 10% of ₹50,000 = ₹5,000 (Section 393, formerly 194J)
- Freelancer receives: ₹54,000 (₹50,000 + ₹9,000 GST − ₹5,000 TDS)
- GST to remit: ₹9,000 (can offset with ITC on business purchases)

### Scenario B: Foreign client pays $1,000 for IT consulting
- GST: Zero-rated (export of service) — 0% if LUT filed
- TDS: Not applicable (foreign payment, no Indian TDS obligation)
- Receive in USD via banking channels within 9 months

### Scenario C: Freelancer pays ₹5,000/month for AWS
- RCM applies: Pay 18% = ₹900 GST to government
- Claim ₹900 as ITC → net cash impact = ₹0 (if outward liability exists)
- Declare in GSTR-3B under reverse charge section

### Scenario D: Freelancer earns ₹10 lakh from professional services (TY 2026-27)
- Presumptive income under Section 58 (44ADA): 50% = ₹5 lakh
- Under New Regime: Tax on ₹5 lakh at applicable slab
- Advance tax: Single payment by March 15, 2027 (100%)
- GST: Mandatory registration if > ₹20 lakh (below threshold here, voluntary)
